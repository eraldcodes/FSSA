Changing Lanes - the Latest Developments in Collateral Management Systems

Robert Palliser, 4sight Sales Director discusses future developments in Collateral Management systems in an interview with Securities Lending Times.

To read the original article download here

SLT: Firstly, can you tell us a little about the history of 4sight and the range of products it provides to the market?

Palliser: 4sight was spun off from OM Technology in 2003. Historically it has been a provider of integrated stock loan and repo systems across all market participant types. This includes direct/agency lenders, niche and global broker dealers etc. Typically, for these institutions, collateral management was a function of the trade life cycle rather than a discipline in itself.

Over the last two years, however, 4sight has become far more involved in collateral management as a stand-alone discipline. This stems from a collateral optimisation, liquidity management and fee generation perspective rather than as a mere risk mitigant.

If we look back two or three years, traders in many houses were tasked to focus their efforts on P&L generation. At the time, there was only a secondary focus on the collateral management of such trades. In this model, managing collateral was frequently seen as a related but autonomous back office operation.

Many securities finance desks had access to large treasury departments able to source term cash on their behalf on an unsecured basis. They were also able to make use of large amounts of unutilized govt. debt holdings with which to collateralize their activities. Balance sheet utilization, liquidity retention and making the optimal return on idle (high quality) inventory were not paramount in many minds.

However, things have changed greatly over the recent past. Our clients are increasingly seeking to optimize their collateral processes. They want to ensure that, wherever possible, they pledge the lowest quality non-cash collateral to their counterparts. This allows them to retain their internal liquidity and make use of their access to Govt debt for firm financing or fee generation purposes.

4sight has been well placed to benefit from this market development. Our modular collateral management system allows users to undertake all aspects of the requisite workflow, from inventory management, through to exposure calculation, collateral allocation, instruction and settlement from a single system.

SLT: Is collateral management still run with a silo-based approach? Or is it now a centralized function within many organizations?

Palliser: Historically it was very silo based. This was as a result of company structure and product lines and the difficulty faced in cross product netting.

It frequently resulted in different departments within a given organization having radically different eligible collateral schedules, minimum margin thresholds and operating processes with the same market counterparts.

However, this silo approach is being rapidly eroded.

We are currently working with a number of clients who have decided to consolidate and centralize their collateral management. Their business model is now focused on ensuring the optimal use of collateral across a range of products. It includes repo, securities lending, on exchange and OTC derivatives.

In this respect, the centralized collateral department is effectively acting as the single interface for their organization with their external counterparts. This is across all relevant products and asset classes. It is also functioning as an internal conduit of collateral to/from a range of departments within their firm.

At 4sight, we are able to structure this activity within our collateral management system. This ensures that the internal sources and recipients of collateral are charged correctly.

Equally importantly, our software helps firms to identify collateral and liquidity surpluses and shortfalls at a company level. They can then act upon them well in advance.

This has a positive impact upon bottom line operating costs for our users. It also ensures that all relevant collateral costs are assigned correctly to underlying trading strategies.

SLT: What market trends or characteristics do you believe have caused firms to look at the centralization of collateral? Do you see these strengthening in the near future?

Palliser:  These are numerous and getting longer by the day.

The Lehman default and the drying up of market liquidity were key drivers of centralization. This increased the need to make better use of finite resource such as capital and balance sheet.

In addition, the desire to better manage counterparty, correlation and operating risk across all collateralized products is integral to any decision to centralize such activity.

There is a significant benefit to bottom line earnings from effective collateral management and collateral optimisation. As firms realize the potential to reduce costs and generate ancillary income, they are quick to embrace this decision. The result is that institutions are now actively seeking technology solutions that can support this model.

SLT: Does moving to an integrated approach change the way firms look at the various risks within collateral management?

Palliser: To some degree - yes. The primary risk focus, regardless of operating model, remains the potential for a given counterparty to default. The correlation of the collateral to that default also continues to be a vital consideration. This hasn't fundamentally changed.

However, the centralized collateral model has highlighted the ability of a firm to look at its various risks across a range of products and on a portfolio basis. Not just counterparty, but also portfolio, liquidity and operating risks.

In this respect, we have increasingly worked with our clients to model their activities and develop "What If" scenarios. Users can run these scenarios in the collateral management system to see the effect of a pre-determined event.

For example, this event could be a counterparty downgrading, or increased market pricing volatility. It could be the general market reluctance to accept a given country's securities or a given asset class etc.

Risk managers within our client firms also want the system to send them automated daily reports to highlight potential warning signs. For example, they want to see whether collateral being pledged to them is unacceptable to their own counterparty base.

They also want to see their exposure to a given country or asset class across their combined portfolios, and/or the size of a given collateral holding versus its free float.

SLT: Is there any difference in collateral management within different areas? For example is collateral management different in say, OTC derivatives compared to securities lending?

Palliser: Historically yes. However our clients appear to be taking the best elements from each area and seeking to make them universal.

For example, securities finance and repo markets used to operate with relatively low minimum margin thresholds. They also made active use of daily margining and the delivery of non-cash collateral.

Conversely, OTC derivative collateral management involved significantly higher thresholds and use of a limited range of collateral. However it allowed for a greater use of dynamic ratings related triggers.

Now, within the centralized collateral model at least, such variances have become noticeably less marked. We have therefore developed support for sophisticated collateral acceptability, concentration limit calculation, and haircut criteria. Users can apply these criteria regardless of the underlying product they are trading.

This in turn is allowing our clients to transform their collateral management activity. Previously it was mainly an operational task, now it is evolving into a liquidity and balance sheet management function.

It is now also a way for them to lower their operating costs and generate incremental revenue through a reduction in financing costs or the creation of additional stock loan fees.

SLT: Does this change the human resource required for collateral management? How does it affect the sales process 4sight undertakes?

Palliser: To some degree - yes, but only in a positive sense.

In the past, the primary function of a collateral manager was to ensure the successful delivery or receipt of collateral to mitigate that day's counterparty exposure.

This often resulted in the unnecessary use of cash and government bonds as collateral. They were easier to move on a same day basis in larger parcels than say corporate bonds and/or equities.

Collateral management systems now allow users to forecast their future collateral needs. Furthermore, they can now also identify potential shortfalls or surpluses in a given asset class or with a given counterpart.

The collateral management focus has therefore been extended from that of exposure mitigation and operational settlement to one of ongoing inventory, liquidity and balance sheet management.

In addition, there is now a good deal of automation in mapping the underlying legal agreements with counterparties. This allows the collateral management system to check the amount, type and quality of inventory that firms can pledge to a given counterparty.

It can then undertake the creation of delivery instructions on an automated basis. However, there will be instances on a daily basis where the collateral manager may need to over-rule its inherent logic.

For example, he/she may be aware that a given security is potentially to be sold or is part of a sensitive trading strategy and is not suitable for pledging. As such, a greater internal knowledge is required of the source and stability of assets.

The ability to immediately drill down on a given security in the collateral management solution and identify its source can help to reduce errors in this respect. The option to see whether a colleague has annotated its potential use elsewhere can also make it easier to avoid conflicts.

Moving onto the sales process, the integrated use of collateral management systems for securities lending, repo, and derivatives collateral management is certainly making the sales process longer and more complex.

This is largely a result of the number of departments involved (traders, collateral managers, risk personnel, operations, regulatory reporting, finance etc.). It is also influenced by the amount of data these users need to extract from the technology system for their day-to-day activities.

But to some degree this is to be expected. Effective collateral management ensures liquidity retention and lower potential operating costs, use of balance sheet and capital. To many firms, it is akin to the "oil within the engine". If managed correctly it will ensure the successful continuation and expansion of their activities.

SLT: Is there a minimum scale a business needs to have before it can optimize its collateral?  Can a client use 4sight for its collateral management activities but retain its existing trading and back office systems?

Palliser: Not really. Regardless of size, leveraged trading firms require the ability to finance their activities in the optimal manner.

The retention of cash and high quality Govt assets - previously used as collateral to third parties and/or exchanges - is thus of paramount importance. This applies whether the client is an internationally renowned global player or a small regional bank or broker.

Collateral optimization is in itself a relatively easy concept to understand. However, it impacts upon all aspects of an organization's activities. This ranges from its ability to finance its ongoing activities, through to counterparty and market risk mitigation.

It also significantly affects the control of operating costs, capital and balance sheet. This is equally important regardless of the size of a client and is perhaps best demonstrated by the spread of enquiries we have received over the past eighteen months.

It is also possible for a client to make use of a collateral/exposure management and optimization system on a stand-alone basis. They can merely integrate data feeds to/from their existing trade capture and settlement instruction delivery systems if required.

However, in many cases projects involve the development of a tailored solution that also makes use of our securities lending and borrowing, and repo systems to closely integrate with the collateral management system.

SLT: Is this centralization and optimization of collateral as relevant and/or prevalent in North America, which has traditionally preferred cash?

Palliser: Previously, the US model has been to lend stock on a callable basis against cash collateral.

However, since Lehman, most US based agent lenders now operate both cash and non-cash collateral programs. US broker dealers are also making use of non-cash collateral in order to minimize their balance sheet usage and for their international activity.

Furthermore, the larger North American broker dealers have substantial OTC client driven and/or proprietary derivative books that they need to collateralize in an optimal manner.

As such, we are fielding more enquiries about our collateral management system from North America than from any other part of the world.

We have therefore broadened our scope to look at the collateralization of ETF creation and FX forwards to complement our clients' traditional OTC derivative activities.

SLT: How much is regulation changing the way firms manage their collateral?

Palliser: Undoubtedly so. The effective use of collateral - in addition to enhancing the bottom line and mitigating risk - can also improve an organization's use of balance sheet and capital.

For example, we are currently developing the ability within our collateral management system to novate bilateral repo trades when passed to a CCP. This will support our clients as they move their activity to the CCP over time.

The main drivers of this the benefits of a reduced risk profile, enhanced netting capabilities and lower capital usage.

Going forward, we anticipate working with our clients to develop a means of calculating where trades could be most optimally written from a balance sheet, collateral cost and capital point of view.

This demonstrates the rapid evolution that is occurring in the market, as our clients seek to optimize the returns on finite resources.

SLT: How automated is the collateral management and collateral optimization process within the 4sight system?

Palliser: Very - the system calculates counterparty exposure on a real time basis. This is as a result of trades being booked into our securities lending or repo systems. Alternatively it is fed in automatically to the collateral management system for OTC derivative transactions.

The user can then access his inventory. As the required legal agreements have already been mapped into the system, the user can also allocate his collateral in line with the prevailing eligible collateral, margining and concentration rule-sets.

Furthermore, we are currently enhancing our collateral management solution to allow users to run "synthetic" allocations. The system bases these allocations on eligibility and concentration criteria and his available inventory.

Users can run this on an intra-day basis to identify any potential collateral shortfalls or surpluses at contract, client, or portfolio level.

Once the user is satisfied that they have made the optimal use of available collateral, the software can automatically undertake the physical allocation of securities.

The system also sends settlement instructions and confirmations to each counterparty. This is across all commonly utilized asset classes, product types and settlement routes.

SLT: How big a transition is it to make from the old way of doing collateral management?

Palliser: Probably far less than market participants would imagine.

Firstly, there are no changes to the underlying legal agreement. There is merely a more detailed mapping of its core terms and an enhanced analysis of the user's available inventory.

The number of collateral movements may increase from the days when large parcels of Govt. debt were delivered for the sake of simplicity.

However, the collateral management system can include all such costs in its collateral optimisation calculations. Moreover, it maintains all standard settlement details and routing preferences. This means that the incidence of fails is very small.

The biggest change is probably in the user mind-set. Collateral managers are now becoming an integral cog in the control of a firm's liquidity, balance sheet and capital.

Whilst operational and settlement knowledge remains vital, collateral management systems now allow users to make greater use of technology to assist them in their collateral management decision process.

SLT: Is this part of the business now considered a cost saving exercise or a profit center?

Palliser: It's both. Firms that have collateralized their activities in a sub-optimal manner in the past, with cash and/or Govt bonds borrowed from internal or external sources, should see an immediate material saving in their daily operating costs.

The enhanced use of previously idle equity and corporate bond inventory that collateral management technology systems allow will free up cash and high quality Govt assets. It is then possible to use these assets in firm/third party financing or fee generating securities finance trades, providing a new revenue stream.

About 4sight Financial Software

4sight Financial Software is an independent software solutions provider to financial institutions, with fifteen years of experience and offices and clients worldwide.
