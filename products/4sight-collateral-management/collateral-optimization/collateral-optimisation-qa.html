Collateral Optimisation Q&A

What issues in collateral optimization does 4sight's solution address?

High quality collateral is becoming more valuable due to regulatory drives in the wake of the financial crisis such as Dodd Frank, Basel III and Emir. These regulations stipulate that banks must hold more high quality liquid assets to act as a buffer and prevent bank failures in the event of another crisis.

They also dictate that standardized derivatives trades must be centrally cleared. The aim of this is to reduce systemic risk, and the central counterparties (CCPs) that these trades must be cleared through will only accept high grade collateral.

This looks likely to create a shortfall in high quality assets, which because of various sovereign downgrades are also dwindling in supply. As a result, financial firms want to hold onto these assets wherever possible - so they need to make sure they are efficient in choosing which collateral they pledge and receive.

There is therefore a strong need for collateral management technology systems that offer collateral optimisation, as the cost savings to financial firms of optimizing their collateral are significant.

There is also a trend towards merging collateral management across business lines to allow greater efficiencies and consolidated views of a firm's risk exposures. These product lines had previously been run in separate silos with individual desks for derivatives, securities lending and repo but in many cases are now being combined.

What does the collateral optimization solution enable 4sight's customers to do that they couldn't do previously?

4sight's collateral optimisation software addresses these issues by allowing users to more accurately calculate the opportunity cost of the collateral that they pledge and receive. This helps traders make better decisions about whether a specific trade is going to be profitable based on the cost of the collateral and allows more intelligent trade pricing.

In addition, the system offers algorithms that can suggest the best collateral to give out, based on each counterparty's acceptability criteria. It also allows users to run 'what-if' scenarios to allocate collateral more effectively to clients. This helps to free up idle assets lying unused on a firms' balance sheet that can be put to use to create an added revenue stream.

Finally and most importantly, our collateral management system allows firms to manage and allocate collateral across multiple business lines including Securities Lending, Repo and OTC/Listed derivatives. This is probably the key differentiator between our collateral management solution and the systems that other vendors offer. Our background is in Securities Lending and Repo technology, so we offer combined functionality in all of these business lines that we believe is unique in the market.

How will the G20 derivatives reforms impact demand for collateral management, and how is 4sight addressing this?

As discussed above, the regulatory reforms that are coming into force are driving a significant demand for more efficient collateral management and optimization.

Some estimates of the global liquidity shortfall resulting from the added regulatory capital banks will need to hold, and the extra margin posted to CCPs run into the trillions of dollars. This means that firms will need to be much more efficient in how they manage their balance sheets and enhance their return on economic capital, so collateral optimization is a key part of this.

In addition, technology systems need to offer connectivity with a range of CCPs and support the complexities of CCP margining on a real time intraday basis. This requires functionality to identify which trades are applicable for CCP margining and model each CCP's margin calculation requirements across all collateralized products.

Systems will also eventually need to support full cross product netting once a standard master netting agreement is formalized. Along with interoperability between CCPs, this will significantly decrease the amount of overall margin posted so it is a welcome development.

Furthermore, our clients require reporting around CCP usage. This ranges from the analysis of collateral cost per transaction/transaction type through to proposing the optimal CCP to use, given available collateral and surplus netting capacity the CCP offers.

We are in close consultation with both clients and prospects on all of these issues and are therefore in a strong position to help our customers adapt to regulatory change and take advantage of the opportunities that will doubtless arise from the new landscape.

In what ways has collateral management changed in the last few years?

Collateral management has effectively moved from a way of mitigating risk to a business opportunity. Sound collateral management is still a powerful way of moderating counterparty credit risk.

However, it has also evolved into a way to boost revenues and reduce costs as pricing of collateral and credit risk becomes more sophisticated. As time goes on it will increasingly become a way for firms to differentiate their offerings in a highly competitive market and is rapidly gaining more and more attention among both buy side and sell side firms as regulatory deadlines move closer.

From a buy side point of view there is also attention on greater segregation of pledged assets as end users seek to ring fence collateral in the event of a broker default (as in the recent case of MF Global for example). Bankruptcy remote collateral will also have a lower risk weighting under Basel III.

Likewise, restrictions around rehypothecation of collateral are also becoming more prevalent following the demise of Lehman Brothers. This should have the effect of reducing the velocity of collateral and further increasing its cost.

From a technology perspective, collateral optimisation is currently the hot topic, and we have seen huge interest in our collateral optimisation solution. Driving this are regulatory demands for banks to hold more capital, coupled with a need to post margin with CCPs as derivatives trading moves to a centrally cleared model.

This is increasing demand for high quality collateral and firms are therefore seeking to use their collateral pools more efficiently. It is also prompting a move to centralize the collateral function across all business lines a firm is involved in, which facilitates a more holistic view of assets and more effective allocation.

Is collateral management a profitable business, a risk mitigation strategy, or both?

Both. We should never detract from the fact that collateral management is primarily a risk mitigation tool and as it evolves, will continue to use ever-more sophisticated methods of assessing counterparty credit risk and managing exposures.

However, due to shortages of high-grade collateral it is also becoming both a cost reduction and a profit generation tool. Successful firms are now pricing and deploying collateral more effectively while also expanding trading opportunities through efficient collateral use and more informed decision-making.

In this sense, collateral management is moving towards becoming a front office trading discipline as well as an operational process. The point should also be made that firms with superior operational capabilities in collateral management can win market share through better client service and more competitive pricing.

How are firms that act across multiple product lines integrating collateral management into their operations?

We have helped a number of clients with this process and there are three elements to successful centralization of collateral management: technology, operations and culture.

This equates to changes in systems, processes and importantly, the mind set of people previously used to working in separate business silos. Firms planning to integrate their collateral management across securities lending, repo and OTC/exchange traded derivatives need to address each of these factors and this can be a complex process.

However, there are significant benefits to centralization. Firstly, because technology systems can now consolidate views of collateral across product lines, users can gain a clearer snapshot of risk across the entire organization or determine net exposures with specific counterparties. This will help firms adapt to regulatory change and reporting more smoothly, for example around the Dodd Frank rules on credit exposure limits.

Secondly, this centralized view of collateral can help drive decisions on the best way to deploy assets based on their opportunity cost and the return on economic capital a given trade can generate. Finally, cross product netting could materialize at some point in the future should agreements for full netting of securities lending, repo and derivatives trades become common.

How should the need for high quality collateral in large quantities be balanced?

While high-grade collateral will most certainly become scarcer, the pain could be eased somewhat by CCPs accepting lower grade assets as collateral and through the use of collateral transformation techniques. There is some debate over whether collateral transformation will be viable for everyone, due to the economics of collateral upgrade trades and associated costs.

The regulatory standpoint on collateral transformation may also influence the shape of the market. Some firms may also simply stop carrying out certain derivatives transactions due to onerous collateral requirements and instead look for alternative methods of trading and hedging risk.

Furthermore, collateral optimisation, interoperability between CCPs and more efficient netting and offsetting processes, particularly across product types could also reduce the need for large amounts of collateral.
